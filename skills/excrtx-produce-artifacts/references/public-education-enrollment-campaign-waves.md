# Public education enrollment campaigns — artifact waves

Use this reference when planning a multi-channel admissions, enrollment, or public-education campaign. The campaign is an interdependent artifact program, not a collection of isolated posts.

## 1. Resolve the operating context before planning pieces

Capture:

- official campaign name;
- campaign, pre-launch, enrollment, and closing dates;
- audiences and relative recruitment difficulty;
- courses/programs actually included;
- geographic reach;
- owned, earned, partner, and paid channels;
- paid-media budget;
- production capacity;
- approver and approval SLA;
- owner of each surface, especially site/hot site/landing page;
- brand, legal, accessibility, image-rights, and electoral-period constraints;
- which facts are confirmed and which depend on an unpublished notice or regulation.

If the user states that they own a surface, record it as canonical responsibility and remove it from open decisions. Assign support roles and a second factual reviewer without transferring ownership.

## 2. Build campaign architecture before content volume

Organize the offer on three axes:

1. **Audience moment:** who the program is for now.
2. **Benefit or professional territory:** what future or job it enables.
3. **Campaign wave:** what decision the audience must make at that point in time.

When many courses share domains, group them into a few meaningful territories rather than creating one disconnected campaign per course. Preserve course-level proof and CTA inside the territory.

## 3. Legitimate artifact gate

An artifact may move from DRAFT to publishable only when it has:

1. official, identifiable factual sources;
2. explicit placeholders for unpublished facts;
3. compliant brand application;
4. neutral public-service language when required;
5. documented rights for image, voice, music, fonts, and other assets;
6. preserved editable source;
7. version, date, owner, and status;
8. second-person factual review;
9. approver sign-off;
10. publication URL, channel, date, and metrics recorded.

Deadline pressure never converts an unverified DRAFT into a legitimate artifact.

## 4. Source-of-truth pattern

Create a source register before production:

```markdown
| Claim/field | Status | Official source | Last checked | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Campaign dates | confirmed | URL/document | YYYY-MM-DD | name/role |
| Application link | placeholder | pending | — | name/role |
| Vacancies | placeholder | pending notice | — | name/role |
```

Use bracketed placeholders such as:

- `[APPLICATION_LINK]`
- `[OFFICIAL_NOTICE]`
- `[VACANCIES_BY_PROGRAM]`
- `[SELECTION_METHOD]`
- `[TEST_DATE_AND_LOCATION]`
- `[CLOSING_TIME]`
- `[RESULTS_SCHEDULE]`
- `[ENROLLMENT_STEPS]`

When the official notice is published, update the register first, then every dependent artifact. Require a second factual check. Retifications follow the same path.

## 5. Recommended artifact register

A class-level campaign commonly needs:

- source and placeholder register;
- strategic brief;
- audience × program × message × proof matrix;
- creative concept and visual direction;
- verbal guide with tone, copy, CTA, and restrictions;
- hot site/landing page source;
- editorial calendar and production board;
- editable social templates;
- scripts, shot lists, raw footage, edit projects, and exports;
- FAQ and messaging-service scripts;
- school/partner kit;
- employer/industry kit;
- public-service press kit;
- launch package;
- paid-media package;
- performance dashboard;
- objection/recovery package;
- final-deadline package;
- final publication and learning register.

Give every artifact an ID, owner, editable source format, due date, dependencies, review state, and publication record.

## 6. Wave model

### Wave 0 — Governance and source of truth

Set up folders, source register, placeholders, naming/version rules, rights records, approval flow, and legal/brand gates.

### Wave 1 — Intelligence, goals, and brief

Gather historical demand, vacancies, audience geography, questions, partner lists, goals, and course-level proof. Finish the master brief and message matrix.

### Wave 2 — Campaign system

Approve concept, verbal guide, visual system, CTA, and representative prototypes before producing volume.

### Wave 3 — Base artifacts

Create the landing preview, calendar, templates, scripts, audiovisual bank, and master pieces. Preserve raw and editable sources.

### Wave 4 — Distribution network

Prepare school, employer, partner, and press kits. Add traceable links and log every contact and publication commitment.

### Wave 5 — Launch readiness

Finish FAQ, messaging flows, launch assets, media assets, dashboard, mobile/desktop checks, link tests, and the end-to-end user journey.

### Wave 6 — Launch and rapid correction

Publish, distribute, activate media, monitor friction, correct the FAQ/landing, and issue a short opening report.

### Wave 7 — Choice and proof

Publish laboratories, projects, students, alumni, professors, services, and program comparisons. Every piece must show evidence and a traceable next step.

### Wave 8 — Objections and recovery

Answer recurring objections, recover interested users, focus channels on low-demand programs, and update reusable FAQ assets.

### Wave 9 — Final conversion

Prepare last-week, 72-hour, 48-hour, and final-day assets. Define closing support, synchronize every channel, and archive URLs, versions, spend, and results.

## 7. Role model

For a small internal team:

- **Campaign director:** strategy, approval, institutional articulation, paid-media decisions, and ownership of any surface explicitly assigned to them.
- **Design/content producer:** calendar, editable templates, adaptations, scheduling, and asset organization.
- **Audiovisual/analytics producer:** capture, editing, captions, raw-source preservation, metrics, and question logging.
- **Exocórtex:** research, brief, copy, scripts, FAQ, releases in DRAFT, consistency review, weekly synthesis, and proposed adjustments.
- **Program coordinators/community:** factual validation, access to projects/labs, and authorized participants.

Separate technical ownership from factual review. A hot-site owner may publish and maintain the page while another person performs the mandatory second check.

## 8. Low-budget media rule

With a small budget, do not split spend across every program. Use a few strong campaign units aligned with recruitment priority, launch/mid/final flights, and retargeting. Let organic channels, partners, schools, and public-service press cover audiences that already have high demand.

## 9. Public-sector and electoral-period gate

Do not infer legal permission from educational merit. When communication occurs during an electoral restriction period:

- keep language impersonal and service-oriented;
- do not attribute institutional results to a government, administration, or authority;
- exclude political slogans, personalities, voices, or symbolic campaign cues;
- publish only confirmed service information;
- validate paid promotion and institutional publicity with the competent communication/legal authority;
- preserve the official legal/reference source in the campaign register.

The plan may define the gate, but it must not present itself as a legal opinion.

## 10. Exit criteria between waves

A wave closes only when:

- expected artifacts exist and open correctly;
- sources and placeholders are registered;
- editable source and exports are preserved;
- factual review passed;
- brand, rights, accessibility, and legal gates passed;
- the approver signed off;
- the next wave has sufficient inputs.

## Pitfalls

- Producing a high volume of posts before approving the campaign system.
- Leaving channel ownership ambiguous after the user has assigned it.
- Treating the hot site as a separate project rather than the campaign’s source and conversion surface.
- Inventing test dates, vacancies, selection rules, quotas, or result schedules before the official notice.
- Using a generic inspirational concept without segment-specific proof.
- Fragmenting a small media budget across too many programs.
- Counting reach as success without application or lead instrumentation.
- Publishing exports without preserving editable sources and authorizations.
